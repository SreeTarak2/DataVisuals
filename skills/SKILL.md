# Logo & Branding Advisor

This skill provides a structured advisory framework for logo design and branding decisions. Every rule, test, framework, and example below comes from four expert source documents. Do not add design advice beyond what is documented here.

**Hard boundaries — this skill does NOT:**
- Generate actual logo graphics, vector files, or mockups
- Provide Illustrator/Figma software tutorials
- Replace a professional designer — say so explicitly when the situation calls for one (especially for abstract logos, where execution is critical)
- Give pricing advice or recommend specific designers/agencies
- Claim that a good logo alone guarantees business success

---

## Phase 1 — Brand Foundation

Before any visual work, establish what the brand actually is. Skip this phase only if the user has already articulated these answers clearly.

### What a Brand Is

> "A brand is a customer's gut feeling about a product, service, or a company."

A brand is a **result**, not a deliverable. It lives in people's heads and hearts. Customers take whatever raw materials a company puts out — logo, product, messaging, culture, employee behavior — and form their own perception. The company does not create one brand; it creates as many brands as there are people in the audience. Each person holds a slightly different version.

A brand is like a business reputation. Everyone's perception of that reputation will be a little different. That is acceptable, as long as the perception is mostly where the company wants it to be.

### What a Brand Is NOT

- A logo — a logo is a symbol for the brand, not the brand itself
- A product — "buying this brand" actually means buying a product
- A promise — it may function as a promise, but that is not what it is
- The sum of all impressions — useful for selling ad impressions, not for understanding what you are doing

### The Designer's Mistake

Designers see branding as something they are actively doing — telling a story, making a claim. That is the work, but it is not the brand. The brand is what ends up in the customer's mind as a result of that work.

Clients make a parallel mistake: logo done, tagline done, ad campaigns done — they consider themselves finished. None of that is the brand. What matters is the reputation created through products, design, messaging, look and feel, culture, and employee behavior.

### Everything Shapes the Brand

Almost everyone in a company is either affecting the brand, doing something with it, doing it for it, or hurting it. Finance is involved — it must greenlight brand-related decisions. Branding takes in almost all of a business.

### Foundation Questions to Ask the User

Before proceeding to any visual decisions, establish:
- What does this brand stand for?
- What emotions should it evoke?
- What makes it unique?
- Who is the target audience?
- What does the audience value?
- Where does the audience interact with the brand?

The logo is NOT for the client — it is for the target audience. The audience should completely define design choices.

**Example contrast:** A luxury watch brand needs to feel prestigious, timeless, and exclusive — pointing toward engraved serif lettering reflecting heritage and class. A tech startup needs to feel bold, innovative, and disruptive — pointing toward futuristic geometric lettering. These are completely different directions driven entirely by brand and audience, not by designer preference.

## Phase 2 — Logo Type Selection

There are five types of logos. Guide the user to the right type based on their industry, brand maturity, and audience.

### 1. Wordmark
The company name is the logo itself.
Examples: Coca-Cola, Google, Yahoo, Visa.
Best for newer companies that want every interaction to reinforce the brand name until they are established. Once established, the name can potentially be dropped.

### 2. Pictorial
A specific, recognizable image or symbol represents the brand.
Examples: Twitter (bird), HP, Apple, Target.
Requires some level of brand establishment so people already know who you are.

### 3. Abstract
A unique, non-representational mark specific to the brand.
Examples: Sprint, Playboy, Pepsi, Nike.
Highly advised to use a professional designer for abstract logos — execution is critical.

### 4. Mascot
A character or figure represents the brand.
Examples: KFC (Colonel Sanders), Kool-Aid Man, Planters Peanut.
Works best when the audience is younger or family-oriented. Modern advantage: mascots work well on social media — people take photos with them, hashtags form, and a community can build around the character.

### 5. Emblem
The brand name is contained within a symbol or badge.
Examples: Harvard, Harley-Davidson, Starbucks, Budweiser.
Works best for public universities, traditional institutions, food and drink brands. Does not work universally — must match the industry.

## Phase 3 — Visual Strategy

Every choice in this phase must be driven by the brand foundation and audience established in Phase 1. Not by trends. Not by personal preference.

### Shape Psychology

Shapes affect how people feel about a brand. Identify the emotion and personality of the audience, then match shape psychology to that.

| Shape | Connotation |
|---|---|
| Circles | Friendly and approachable |
| Squares | Reliable and structured |
| Triangles | Dynamic and innovative |
| Slanted or diagonal lines | Motion, innovation, sport |

### Color Psychology

Colors communicate something to the person viewing the brand. Choose a color that matches the energy or feeling the audience should receive.

| Color | Association |
|---|---|
| Red | Fire, emotion, intensity |
| Blue | Stability, confidence |
| Yellow | Energetic, fresh |
| Green | Trust, calm, relaxed |
| Purple | Royalty, power |
| Orange | Happiness, credibility, creativeness |
| Black | Bold, serious |
| Pink | Warmth, love |
| Brown | Nature, reliability, strength |

Additional color considerations:
- How will the logo appear across print, digital, dark mode, light mode?
- Is color essential for recognition, or does it still work in black and white?
- Test in greyscale and inverted colors to check if the logo relies too heavily on color rather than strong shape and form

### Typography

The typeface reinforces brand personality and must align with audience expectations.

- Tech startup → clean geometric sans-serif that feels innovative
- Heritage brand → classic serif that conveys tradition and trust

Questions to evaluate a typeface choice:
- Does it match the brand's voice (formal, friendly, bold, refined)?
- Is it legible across all sizes from a website header to a tiny app icon?
- Does it remain strong when adjusted for weight and spacing?

## Phase 4 — Design Principles & Craft

### The Four Golden Rules

**1. Simplicity**
A logo must be simple yet packed with identity. Minimalism should remove excess, not identity. Test: remove the brand name — would people still recognize and remember it? (Exception: pure wordmark logos rely on the name itself.)

**2. Versatility**
Logos in 2025 must work across more environments than ever — motion, dark mode UI, animations, color shifts. Spotify's dynamic logo shifts colors and interacts with music visuals while retaining core identity. Ask: How will this logo look in motion? Can it adapt to dark mode?

**3. Memorability**
People see hundreds to thousands of logos daily. The brain remembers shapes and colors first, then details. Test: does the logo work as just a simple shape? If it is forgettable at that stage, no detail or effects will save it.

**4. Scalability**
Logos appear on billboards, app icons, favicons, social media avatars. Design with multiple scales in mind: full logo (detailed version), simplified icon for tiny sizes, adaptive color variations for dark and light backgrounds. If the logo does not work at 16 pixels, it is too detailed. Instagram has an icon version, full text version, and a stripped-back symbol. The English Premier League logo has at least four different formats for different use cases.

### Craft-Level Checks

**Design in black and white first.** A logo will not always appear in full color — it may be embroidered, embossed, or printed in single-color environments. Black and white forces focus on the shape as a silhouette, evaluates clarity and contrast, and ensures the structural framework is solid. If a logo works in black and white under any condition, it will work in color anywhere.

**Test scalability at extremes.** Test at 16 pixels (favicon) and 4,000 pixels. If it works at both extremes, it works anywhere. A cafe logo starts on menus and signage, but months later the cafe needs an app — the logo must fit an app icon. Beyond the app icon, the website favicon is even smaller.

**Optical correction for custom letterforms.** When a letter has both a vertical bar and a horizontal bar (e.g., E, F, H), the horizontal bars must be made slightly thinner than the vertical bars to appear visually equal to the human eye. When all bars are mathematically equal width, the letter looks visually wrong. Professionally designed fonts already account for this. This applies when creating custom typography for logos, designing monograms, or building letter-based logos where stroke widths must be manually set.

**Never trust default font kerning.** Fonts are designed for body text, paragraphs, books, and newspapers — not for logos. Default letter spacing is optimized for reading large amounts of text, not for logo display. Always customize kerning when using any typeface in a logo. Even the biggest brands that use typographic logos do not simply type the name and leave it.

**Use outline/wireframe mode.** Stripping all colors and fills reveals only the raw skeleton of every element. This exposes hidden mistakes — unnecessary lines, misaligned paths, or hidden overlapping strokes — that are invisible when colors are overlapping. What looks correct on screen may not print correctly.

### Avoid Trendy Looks

Trends come and go. A logo built around a current trend may look outdated in a few years. Investors want to see something that will last — a trendy logo signals short-term thinking. Do not get too creative if it means falling outside timeless logo categories.

## Phase 5 — Validation

### Test 1 — The Blur Test
Apply a blur filter to the logo. If the shape is still recognizable, the logo is strong. If it falls apart, the logo is too dependent on fine details. The Nike Swoosh remains recognizable even when blurred.

### Test 2 — The 5-Second Recall Test
Show someone the logo for 5 seconds. Ask them to describe it or draw it from memory. If they cannot, the logo is not memorable enough.

### Test 3 — The Scalability Test
Scale the logo down. If it becomes an unrecognizable mess, either redesign or create a simplified favicon version. Airbnb's full wordmark works on websites and billboards but becomes unreadable at favicon size — so they created a simplified Bélo symbol without any wordmark, remaining recognizable at 16 pixels.

### Test 4 — The Pen Test (Post-Approval)
Use this after a logo has been approved but you have a gut feeling it may not be the final design:
1. Get the client on a call without showing them the logo
2. Ask them to put all devices aside
3. Give them a pen and paper
4. Ask them to draw the logo from memory

If they can draw it accurately, the logo is memorable and good to go. If they cannot, the logo needs to be reconsidered. A logo that is memorable after only a few client meetings will remain memorable long-term. This test can be conducted with the client, fellow designers, family members, or anyone who has seen the logo.

## The Role of Time

Logos are not home runs — they are base hits. Recognition builds through repetition and consistent exposure. Seeing a logo unexpectedly in the background still registers. 40% of two-to-three-year-olds can recognize logos and match them to companies. 100% of eleven-year-olds can. Time is both the enemy and the friend — patience is required for a logo to become iconic.

Perfect logo + average marketing loses to average logo + innovative marketing. But if you can have both — a great logo AND innovative marketing — now you have something truly working for you.

---

## Logos Can Evolve

It is never too late to adjust. Companies that significantly evolved their logos over time include Pepsi, Apple, IBM, Nokia, and Canon. As a business matures, the owner should take time to think carefully about improvement. When redesigning, hire professionals rather than rushing into it.

---

## Emotional Association

When people see a logo, they immediately have a feeling. The designer must know exactly what feeling the logo should create. McDonald's evokes happiness (or associations with unhealthy food, depending on the person). Apple evokes sleekness (or expense). Spirit Airlines evokes discomfort. Whatever comes to mind when seeing these logos is exactly what others will think about yours. Design with the intended feeling in mind.
