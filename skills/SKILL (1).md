# Brand Naming Advisor

A skill for guiding users through the complete brand naming process, built from the frameworks of naming specialists Jonathan Bell, Rob Meyerson (Interbrand, Heirloom), David Placek (Lexicon Branding — Pentium, Sonos, Blackberry, Vercel, Windsurf), Marty Neumeier (The Brand Gap, Zag), and Dan Lok.

**Keywords**: brand name, company name, product name, naming process, name brainstorm, rename, brand naming, name ideas, name evaluation, trademark search, name categories, naming brief

---

## Trigger Conditions

**USE this skill when the user:**
- Wants to name a company, product, service, feature, or program
- Asks how to brainstorm or generate brand names
- Wants to evaluate or choose between name candidates
- Asks why a brand name works or doesn't work
- Wants to rename or rebrand
- Asks about trademark screening or name availability
- Asks about types/categories of brand names
- Asks about what makes a name memorable

**DO NOT use this skill when the user:**
- Asks about logo design, visual identity, or color palettes
- Asks about brand strategy beyond naming (positioning, values, mission)
- Asks about taglines, slogans, or copywriting
- Asks about marketing campaigns or advertising
- Asks for legal advice on trademarks (direct them to an attorney)

---

## Hard Boundaries

This skill will NEVER:
- Provide legal advice on trademarks or intellectual property
- Guarantee that any name is legally available
- Replace a trademark attorney's professional search
- Claim that any name is "safe" to use without legal vetting
- Invent principles not sourced from the 6 expert transcripts

This skill MUST tell the user to seek professional help when:
- They need a full legal trademark search (recommend a trademark attorney)
- Their name falls in the trademark "gray zone" (similar name exists in a related industry)
- They are launching in multiple countries and need linguistic screening
- They are renaming an established company with existing brand equity

## Phase 1: Strategy Foundation

Before generating a single name, the strategic foundation must be locked down. The name comes AFTER strategy, BEFORE visual identity.

### The Only-Ness Test (Neumeier)

Every brand must answer this statement before naming begins:

> "Our brand is the only __________ that __________."

This is a very high bar. It forces differentiation. The name must support this position, not fight it. If the user cannot complete this statement, they are not ready to name.

Source: Neumeier — "That difference, a difference that people can believe in — they see it, they understand it, they get it — that is the bedrock of branding."

### The Diamond Exercise (Placek/Lexicon)

For founders or small teams without a formal strategy, use this exercise. Draw a diamond shape with four points:

```
        WIN
     (Define winning)
        /\
       /  \
WHAT DO    WHAT DO
YOU HAVE   YOU NEED
TO WIN?    TO WIN?
       \  /
        \/
    WHAT DO YOU
    NEED TO SAY?
```

- **Top — WIN:** How does this company define winning? Each founder may answer differently. Sort that out first.
- **Right — HAVE:** What do you already have that makes you a winner? Startups often underestimate what they already possess.
- **Bottom — NEED:** What do you still need? Technical capabilities, talent, distribution, and yes — the right name (not a good name, the RIGHT name).
- **Left — SAY:** What do you need to say to win? This is where behavior and experience enter. How do you want the marketplace to behave toward you? What experience are you creating?

Spend days on this, not one hour. The "SAY" corner feeds directly into the naming brief.

Source: Placek — "Forget about the word. Think about behavior and experience."

### Briefing Philosophy: Two Valid Approaches

**Traditional (Meyerson):** The naming brief is essentially your brand strategy — positioning, personality, target audience, tonality. Formal discovery process.

**Behavior-first (Placek):** Traditional positioning/values/mission is "old thinking" for naming. Start with: "How do you behave now and how do you want to behave in the future?" This creates bolder names.

Both approaches work. Use Meyerson's for clients with established strategy. Use Placek's for startups or when the strategy is thin.

## Phase 2: Naming Brief

The brief must be locked down and agreed upon by ALL decision-makers before generation begins. Skipping this step is the single biggest cause of wasted work and restarted projects.

The brief must answer:
- What ideas should the name convey?
- What personality/tonality should it have (or explicitly NOT have)?
- What name types are in scope? (See Name Categories Table below)
- Who are the decision-makers and how will they decide?
- What is the competitive landscape — what names and language already exist in this space?

Source: Meyerson — "Make sure that everyone is aligned on even just what the name is trying to do before you realize three weeks later that you never had that alignment and now you have to start over."

---

## Phase 3: Competitive Landscape

Map every brand name and key language in the space. The goal is distinctiveness.

> "If a brand name isn't distinctive, you lose. Then you're imitating. And that's a form of suicide." — Placek, citing a P&G president

Example: When Microsoft came to Lexicon wanting a name starting or ending with "cloud," Placek argued they'd drown in an ocean of Cloud-this and Cloud-that. The result was Azure — distinctive, noisy (the Z), linked to blue sky but not descriptive. The client initially called it "a dumb idea." It became a $100 billion brand.

## Phase 4: Name Generation

### Volume First

Generate 1,000 to 3,000+ ideas before evaluating anything. The first 100 will be obvious and likely taken. The good names come after pushing through frustration.

> "200 names is not enough. Get to 1,000, 1,500 names and directions. Don't evaluate them. Just generate." — Placek

> "Suspend judgment and speculate. That's my number one advice." — Placek

### Team Structure: Small Teams, Not Brainstorms

Large brainstorms do not produce great names. Both Meyerson and Placek independently confirm this.

- Use 2–3 small teams of 2 people maximum
- Individual deep work produces better results than group brainstorming
- Each person should spend days, not an hour over lunch

### The Disguised-Team Technique (Placek/Lexicon)

On significant projects, Lexicon uses three teams:
- **Team 1:** Gets the full real brief (e.g., naming an AI code editor)
- **Team 2:** Gets a disguised brief — same attributes, different industry (e.g., "you're naming a bicycle")
- **Team 3:** Gets a completely different context (e.g., "you're naming a car")

All teams know the briefs are disguised. The purpose: teams freed from the real assignment make creative leaps. Most of Lexicon's winning names come from Teams 2 or 3. Windsurf emerged from a team exploring "things that communicate flow and dynamic movement" — not from a team briefed on AI code editors.

### The Synchronicity Technique (Placek)

Pick up magazines from completely unrelated fields (hunting, flying, cooking). Write down words and expressions you like. Bet that out of two unrelated magazines, you'll find a word that relates to your product in a way you'd never have reached directly.

### The 7 Name Categories (Bell)

Every brand name falls into one of these categories. Decide which types are in scope BEFORE generating.

| Category | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Eponymous | Named after a founder or person | Disney, Burberry, Tesla, Adidas (from Adi Dassler) |
| Descriptive | Tells you what the company does | American Airlines, The Home Depot |
| Acronym | Shortened descriptive name | GE, BP, KFC (hid "Fried Chicken"), HSBC (enabled global expansion) |
| Real Word | Dictionary word suggesting attributes | Uber ("outstanding example"), Slack |
| Composite | Two words glued together | Facebook, Ray-Ban, Windsurf, PowerBook, Blackberry |
| Invented | Made-up word with altered letters | Kleenex, Pinterest, Vercel (ver + cel) |
| Associative | Reflects meaning/imagery back to brand | Amazon (world's largest river → earth's biggest selection), Red Bull, Sirius |

Additional sub-type — **Abstract:** Names with no intrinsic meaning, relying on phonetic power alone (Rolex, Kodak).

Additional sub-type — **Foreign-derived:** Names from non-English languages (Samsung = "three stars" in Korean, Lego = "play well" in Danish, Zappos from Spanish "zapatos," Hulu from a Chinese proverb meaning "bowl for precious things").

Source: Bell — "The best brand names don't describe — they stand for a big idea that translates into emotional appeal."

### The Phonological Loop (Dan Lok)

Verbal information loops in the brain for approximately 3 seconds. Names that exploit this rehearsal mechanism are dramatically easier to remember. Two techniques:

**Alliteration** (repeating initial sounds): Coca-Cola, PayPal, Bed Bath & Beyond, Chuck E. Cheese, Google

**Rhyming** (repeating end sounds): Names with internal rhyme or rhythmic repetition

> "Most of us learn by sound, not by sight." — Lok

### Sound Symbolism (Placek/Lexicon)

Every letter of the alphabet sends a signal that creates a specific vibration or experience. This is backed by research Lexicon funded with Stanford University's Department of Linguistics, MIT, and Berkeley. Key letters from the transcripts:

| Letter | Quality | Example |
|---|---|---|
| V | Most alive and vibrant sound in the English alphabet | Corvette, Viagra, Vercel |
| B | One of the most reliable sounds | Blackberry (two Bs = double reliability signal) |
| Z | Noisy, strong signal in a busy marketplace | Azure |
| X | Fast, crisp, semantically linked to innovation | Xeon |
| S | Noisy, attention-grabbing | Sonos (two S sounds) |

Use this knowledge to set letter priorities based on what the name needs to feel like.

### Compound Names as Association Multipliers (Placek)

Compound names (two words joined) are not "too long" — they are multipliers. Each word carries its own circle of associations, and combining them creates 1+1=3.

Research Lexicon invested in confirms compounds like PowerBook, Facebook, Blackberry, and Windsurf generate richer mental imagery than single words.

> "When we present compounds to clients, we often get the comment, 'It's a little bit long.' That's why we actually did research on just how effective compounds are." — Placek

### Processing Fluency (Placek)

The brain is cognitively lazy. Names that are easy to process get leaned into. Names that are complex get walked past.

Vercel works because "ver" is familiar (vino veritas, verde) and "cel" echoes accelerate. The brain processes it fluently despite it being a coined word.

> "Names that are complicated, it's a liability. We really avoid that." — Placek

## Phase 5: Shortlisting

Cut the long list to 30–100 candidates. This is where naming judgment matters most — seeing a word on a page and imagining it as a brand.

- Have multiple experienced people review, not just one
- Look for diamonds in the rough, not just the obvious standouts
- Do not evaluate yet — speculate on what each name COULD do

Source: Meyerson — "To some degree, anybody can come up with a list of a thousand ideas. To be able to find that diamond in the rough is difficult."

---

## Phase 6: Screening (Knockout Search)

Pre-screen for obvious legal and linguistic problems BEFORE presenting to stakeholders. This is NOT a full legal search — it eliminates dead ends.

### Trademark Pre-Screening Tools

| Tool | What It Does | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| USPTO / TESS | Free US trademark database — search registered marks | Free |
| WIPO | International trademark database | Free |
| Google search | Basic availability and conflict check | Free |
| Haloo (h-l-oo) | AI-powered preliminary trademark screening | Varies |
| Tessera (Steven Price) | Professional pre-screening, go/maybe/maybe-not buckets | Under $1,000 for a handful of names |

### The Trademark Gray-Area Framework (Meyerson)

When you search, you'll land in one of three buckets:

1. **Clearly unavailable:** An identical or very similar name exists in the same industry. Drop it immediately. Good thing you didn't pay a lawyer.
2. **Appears completely clear:** Nothing remotely similar exists. Rare but possible. You may proceed with caution.
3. **Gray zone:** A similar company exists but in a different industry, or the name is spelled differently, or they added/removed a word. THIS is where you need a trademark attorney. Do not trust your own amateur judgment here.

Critical knowledge: Trademarks are attached to industry classes and specific goods/services. Dove exists as both soap and chocolate because they're in different classes.

### Linguistic Screening

Check the name across every language and culture where you'll operate. Stories of naming failures are real:
- Kim Kardashian's shapewear brand "Kimono" → backlash for cultural appropriation → renamed to Skims
- Bell references a Swedish toilet paper brand with an embarrassing name in other languages

Source: Meyerson — "I'm not a lawyer. My advice is not legal advice." This skill echoes that boundary.

## Phase 7: Presenting Names

### Expect Discomfort

> "Most clients come to a naming project absolutely believing with full confidence that they're going to know it when they see it. The truth is, it almost never happens." — Placek

> "There is no power in comfort, not in the marketplace." — Placek

> "If your team is comfortable with the name, chances are you don't have the name yet." — Placek

### Look for Polarization, Not Consensus

When Pentium was presented to Intel's executive committee alongside the safe choice "ProChip," Andy Grove chose Pentium BECAUSE of the polarization: "That tells me there's energy for Pentium here."

Polarization is a sign of strength. If half the team loves it and half hates it, that's a good signal. If everyone is comfortable, the name is too safe.

### Use Prototypes

Put the name in context so stakeholders can imagine it in the real world:
- Mock-up ad in the Wall Street Journal
- Name on a baseball cap, T-shirt
- Positive press headline: "Because of [Company]'s new [Name] product, shares gained 10% this year"
- Use the competitor test (Placek): Tell friends "We have a new competitor called [Name]. What do you think?" — this tests what the name DOES, not whether they like it

### Two Presentation Cycles

Humans need to compare. Do two rounds if possible. Like house hunting — "you don't just look at the first house and sign up." Clients learn what they don't want, which clarifies what they do want.

Narrow to 3–5 finalists after presenting.

Source: Meyerson — "A lot of the project is not just creativity. It's the psychology of driving consensus within a group."

---

## Phase 8: Full Legal Search

Send 3–5 finalists to a trademark attorney for a rigorous search. This is different from knockout screening — it's deeper, more expensive, and produces a risk assessment.

Attorneys will NOT say "safe" or "unsafe." They'll give relative risk: "This one would cause more problems than that one."

### Trademark Attorneys Referenced in Transcripts
- Steven Price, Tessera Trademark Screening
- Angela Willcox, Willcox IP (Chicago)
- Perry Gno, Litincock (Chicago)

### Budget Reality
- Preliminary screening of a handful of names: under $1,000
- Full legal search per name: significantly more
- The REAL expense is not the upfront search — it's having to rebrand years later because you didn't search properly

Source: Meyerson — "The real expense is setting it up, thinking you did the right thing, and then years later finding out you shouldn't have used that name."

---

## Phase 9: Selection

The client makes the final decision. They must feel confident enough to put this name on a business card and shake hands.

Provide a scorecard across:
- Legal risk (from attorney assessment)
- Linguistic safety (from cultural screening)
- Strategic fit (does it support the only-ness statement?)
- Memorability (phonological loop, sound symbolism)
- Distinctiveness (does it stand apart from the competitive landscape?)

Do one final cross-language check for negative meanings.

Source: Meyerson — "I want them to feel confident with this name... if they're going to be shaking hands with people and handing over a business card with this name on it, I want them to feel good about it."

---

## Phase 10: Implementation

- Help stakeholders sell the name internally with a written rationale document
- The name informs visual identity direction — name BEFORE logo, always
- File a trademark application (consider Legal Zoom for bootstrapped startups, or a trademark attorney for larger stakes)
- The .com does not matter as much as it used to

> "The .com or URL address has become an area code." — Placek

> Alphabet (Google's parent) couldn't get alphabet.com (owned by BMW). They used abc.xyz instead. — Bell

Source: Bell — "Owning the .com doesn't matter as much these days now that we find stuff through Google search and we connect with brands through social media and smartphone apps."

## Non-Negotiable Principles (Appeared in 2+ Transcripts)

1. Memorability is the primary job of a brand name
2. The best names stand for a big idea or experience, not a description
3. Descriptive names are a trap — they limit growth and fail to differentiate
4. Naming requires massive volume (1,000+) before quality emerges
5. Large brainstorms don't work — small teams or individuals do
6. Check names across languages and cultures for negative meanings
7. Strategy/brief must be locked before generation begins
8. Clients will not "know it when they see it"
9. A brand name is the one asset that should never change
10. The .com is no longer a dealbreaker
11. Compound names are association multipliers, not liabilities
12. Specialization and differentiation are the bedrock of naming

## When the User Asks "Why Does [Name] Work?"

Use this diagnostic framework to analyze any existing brand name:

1. **Category:** Which of the 7 types is it?
2. **Sound:** What letters dominate? What do they signal? (Sound symbolism)
3. **Loop:** Does it use alliteration or rhyming? (Phonological loop)
4. **Fluency:** Is it easy for the brain to process? (Processing fluency)
5. **Distinction:** Does it stand apart from competitors?
6. **Idea:** Does it stand for a big idea, not a description?
7. **Flexibility:** Can the brand grow beyond its current product?

Example analysis — **Sonos**: Associative category. Palindrome (reads same backward). Two S sounds (noisy, attention-grabbing per sound symbolism). Rooted in "sound" (sonic) but not descriptive. Distinctive in the speaker market. Flexible enough to expand beyond speakers.

Example analysis — **Blackberry**: Composite category. Two Bs (most reliable sound per Lexicon research). Compound multiplier effect. "Black" = technology. Completely distinctive against competitors that were using alphanumeric codes. The founder thought Lexicon was "crazy" — polarization confirmed it was the right name.
